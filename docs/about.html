Fire Flow Rates

Fire flow determines the amout of water a given fire requires to maintain control. Two formulas are used to calulate these values, NFA Academy, and Iowa University.


Iowa Rate of Flow

The Iowa Rate of Flow is based on work performed by Keith Royer and Bill Nelson while they were working for Iowa State University’s fire training program. While studying the effects of fog streams on interior fires, the pair demonstrated that all you need to do is put the right amount of water in the right place for the fire to go out.

The Iowa formula is: Required volume = (length x width x height) ÷ 100

Example: The same 30' x 50' building used in the first example is 10 feet high. 30 x 50 x 10 = 15,000, divided by 100 = 150 gpm.

National Fire Academy (NFA)

The NFA formula is based on offensive interior operations where less than 50 percent of the building is involved.

The formula is: Needed fire flow = [(length x width) ÷ 3] x percent of involvement

Example: For a 30' x 50' building that’s 20 percent involved, the flow would be 30 x 50 = 1,500 square feet, divided by 3 = 500 x 0.20 = 100 gpm.

Calulator Inputs

  • Building Length (feet, integer)
  • Building Width (feet, integer)
  • Building Height (feet, ingeter)
  • Percent Involved (10,...100)

Water Supply

This calculator determines how long a particular Engine and Tender combination can pump water on a fire without being resupplied.


Water Supply Computation

The formula is straight forward: time = (gallons of water / pump-rate)

Example: A tender holds 3000(gal) ofwater, engine 750(gal) and the engine pump rate of 200 gpm at 200psi. 200 psi is the industry standard pressure rating for ISO testing. Engines must maintain this pumprate for at least 20 minutes. In the Exampel above: ((3000+750)/200) = 18.5 minutes.
If time is < 20.0 minutes, resupply warning message is displayed.

Calulator Inputs

  • Tender Capacity in Gallons
  • Engine Capacity in Gallons
  • Pump Rate @ 200Psi
